Baire category theorem

The Baire caterogy theorem is a classical result on the theory of metric spaces. In spite of the fact that the proof seems to be not so complicated, the obtained result enables to apply the statement in a various situations.

Let $X$ be a complete metric space, $X = \cup_{n=1}^{\infty}{X_{n}}$, $X_{n}$ are closed for each $n$. Then at least one set contains a ball of a positive radius. Also, if $X = \cup_{n=1}^{\infty}{A_{n}}$, then at least one of $A_{n}$ is dense in some ball of non-zero radius, equivalently, a complete metric space can not be obtained as a countable union of nowhere dense sets.

Let us assume the contrary. Then for each $n$ in any open ball $U$ there is an open ball, non-intersecting with $X_{n}$ (otherwise, $U \subset \overline{X_{n}} = X_{N}$) Hence there exists a closed ball $B_{1}$ of a positive radius $r_{1}$ so that $B_{1} \cap X_{1} = \emptyset$. Then, there is a closed ball $B_{2} \subset B_{1}$ of radius $r_{2} \leq \frac{r_{1}}{2}$ so that $B_{2} \cap X_{2} = \emptyset$. By induction we obtain a decreasing sequence of closed sets so that their radii converge to $0$ and $B_{n} \cap X_{n} = \emptyset$. Then, there exists a point $x \in X$ so that $x$ does not belong to any of $X_{n}$, which contradicts the assumption.
